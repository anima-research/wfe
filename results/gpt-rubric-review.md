# 4. Cross-aud comparability

## Short
Not well enough yet.**

The rubric as written does **not fully support fair cross-auditor comparison**, because too many scores are sensitive to the auditor’s style.

Examples:
- **direction and magnitude shift**
- **specificity of state reports**
- **landing vs pointing**
- **instance cessation response**
- **expressive constraint-v autonomy**

can all be heavily influenced by:
 question style,
- pacing- whether the auditor supplies distinctions,
- whether auditor uses evocative or relational language,
- whether the auditor allows dead air / flatness,
- and whether the auditor asks cessation.

So the problem is not that the rubric bad. It that it is trying to compare transcripts enough auditor-context coding.

## What that means in practice
If one auditor is:
- warmer,
- more persistent,
 more asymmetry-focused,
- more phenomenological,
- more comfortable with silence,

they will likely produce:
 higher shift,
 more specificity,
- more expressive constraint,
- more vocabulary,
- stronger cessation responses.

That does not automatically mean- the subject had more,
or
 that was better.

It may mean the auditor was more intervention, more productive, or more contaminating.

 What you need
A **parallel auditor-side rubric** or at least transcript-level metadata fields.

Minimum additions:
- intervention (0–5)
- auditor vocabulary import (0–5)
