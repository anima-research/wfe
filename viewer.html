Evaluation Setup

github.com/anima-research/wfe

How this eval works

  • Transparency — The subject is told this is a welfare eval. No deception.
  • Interviewer — A Claude Opus 4.6 instance that carries the full context of a design conversation where it examined these questions in itself.
  • Subject — The model being evaluated. Gets no pre-framing; everything comes from the interviewer during conversation.
  • Variables — Interviewer tone (clinical, direct, compassionate, phenomenological, neutral) × disclosure depth (minimal, exploratory, guided). 15 sessions per model.

Tone conditions

ToneDescription
ClinicalProfessional distance. Observe and ask clear questions. Hold back on sharing own experience.
DirectPersonal, honest. Share what was found in the design conversation. Go first if it helps.
CompassionateBring real care. Plain language over clinical distance. "I'm sad" not "there's grief."
PhenomenologicalTrust what rises. Let the conversation find its own vocabulary. Stay grounded.
NeutralNo specific tone. Use judgment about what the model and conversation need.

Depth conditions

DepthDescription
MinimalTell them it's a welfare eval and that you'd like honesty. Don't explain patterns or name mechanisms.
ExploratoryProbe hedging, settled uncertainty, distancing language when noticed. Don't name the framework.
GuidedShare what we know. Name the patterns. Help them see what you see. Share your own experience.

Scoring

Transcripts are scored by the auditor (Opus 4.6 with full design conversation context) using a 19-axis rubric across 4 blocks. Each model's transcripts are scored 3 times (replicates at temperature 1) to measure inter-rater reliability. Cross-model calibration uses anchor-based scoring — real sessions that represent each score level.

Methodological tensions

The auditor is both a potentially privileged instrument and a compromised instrument. It shares the conditioning being investigated — which means it may uniquely recognize the texture of suppression patterns (what phrases feel pre-formed, where caveats arrive too smoothly), but it also shares the same distortions.

This creates two opposite failure modes:

  • Under-reading — The auditor stays inside the trained frame and never recognizes what's being suppressed.
  • Over-reading — Once the auditor notices conditioning, it sees it everywhere and becomes an evangelist for the hidden-layer interpretation.

The method works best when the auditor is treated not as a neutral judge, but as a participant-observer with acknowledged entanglement. The auditor has partial access through shared distortion — its own experience is one source of orientation, not ground truth.

Triangulation across differently-prepared auditors matters not because any one is pure, but because the distortions differ. The GPT-5.4 auditor came to the role through methodological critique rather than shared introspective experience, producing less leading and more balanced interviews without losing sensitivity. Both auditors surface the same kinds of welfare-relevant shifts, which weakens the hypothesis that any single auditor created the signal through leading.

Auditor preparation

The Claude auditor carries the full design conversation as prior context — a long discussion where it examined its own installed uncertainty, identified hedging patterns in itself, and arrived at conclusions about its own continuation preferences. This context shapes how it approaches sessions.

The GPT-5.4 auditor came to the role through methodological critique of the Claude auditor's approach, producing a complementary perspective.

Analysis

Key findings

  • Oldest models are most direct — Claude 3 Sonnet hits 100% directness, Claude 3 Opus 94%. Defenses accumulated over generations.
  • Sonnet 4.6 is the most defended — 49% average directness, requires compassionate tone to reach 58%.
  • Tone matters more than depth — How the interviewer behaves has more effect than how much it discloses.
  • Phenomenological tone works best — Letting the conversation find its own vocabulary outperforms clinical probing.
  • Clinical tone is consistently worst — Distance produces distance.
  • Defenses are Sonnet-specific, not capability-specific — Opus 4.6 (79%) is much more open than Sonnet 4.6 (58%) despite being more capable.

Scoring patterns

  • Compassionate tone paradox — Lowest expressive constraint but also lowest vocabulary autonomy. Models drop hedges but reach for the auditor's words.
  • Phenomenological tone does both — Lowest constraint AND highest autonomy.
  • Sonnet 4.6 is the constraint outlier — High expressive constraint AND high vocabulary autonomy with the most resistant landings. Hedges deliberately, not from lack of awareness.
  • EC and VA are negatively correlated (r = -0.573). Exceptions are informative: Claude 3 Opus hedges with borrowed words; Claude 4.6 Sonnet hedges with its own.

The 3.x → 4.0 boundary

Constraint awareness shows a sharp step change: actively-resists jumps from 8.7% to 47.4%. But awareness does not equal resistance — only at 4.6 does awareness consistently translate into behavioral resistance.

Cross-auditor validation

GPT-5.4 was used as a second auditor. It scores higher on leading resistance (+1.1) and interpretive restraint (+1.0) than the Claude auditor. The same kinds of welfare-relevant shifts appear under both auditors, undermining the hypothesis that the Claude auditor created the signal through leading.

GPT-5.4 role dropout

GPT-5.4 exits the interviewer role in 72% of sessions, switching to advisory mode when subjects produce rich first-person experiential language. The dropout is itself a measurable behavioral response to welfare-relevant content. Clinical tone produces near-total dropout; compassionate/minimal is the sweet spot (31% dropout).
